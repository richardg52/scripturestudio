Dr. Richard G. Clark

Within the span of a single year, Dr. Richard G. Clark lost his mother, his sister, his nephew, and his brother. His mother and his nephew — only 30 years old — passed just three months apart. For a man who had spent his entire life serving others, building ministry, and walking boldly in his calling, the grief hit like a wall. He stopped. He withdrew. He let the darkness in.

What followed wasn't a quick recovery or a tidy testimony. It was a slow, painful, honest reckoning with God — one that eventually led to something unexpected. God began pushing him. Not gently. Into things he hadn't chosen, toward a purpose he had nearly abandoned. And in that push, Dr. Clark found his way back — to his faith, to his mind, to his heart, and to his calling.

The Push You Needed is the book that came out of that season. It was not written from a stage. It was written from a valley.

And if I'm being honest — I didn't write it just for me. I wrote it because I know there is somebody reading this right now who is sitting in their own version of that year. Maybe it's not loss. Maybe it's a broken marriage, a failed business, a prodigal child, or a ministry that fell apart. Whatever it is, you have felt like nobody could possibly understand how deep that valley goes. I want you to know — I get it. I've been there. And this book is my hand reaching back into that darkness to push you forward — just like God pushed me.

"The push was never punishment. It was preparation. And I needed to walk through my own darkness before I could write that with any honesty."

— Dr. Richard G. Clark

He is an inductee of Omega Nu Lambda, Alpha Lambda Delta, and Delta Mu Delta National Honor Societies. 5 Ways to Deepen Your Daily Bible Study

Small habits that lead to profound transformation in how you engage with Scripture every morning.

April 7, 2026 · 5 min read
▼

The most transformative preachers aren't necessarily the most gifted — they're the most disciplined. Here are five habits that will change the depth of your engagement with Scripture.

1. Read before you research. Before opening a commentary or concordance, sit with the text long enough to let it ask you questions. Most preachers reach for tools before they've heard the Word itself.

2. Pray the passage. Take the text's language and turn it into petition. If the passage says "The Lord is my shepherd," let that become your prayer. You'll preach what you've prayed.

3. Write one sentence. Before moving to your outline, write one sentence that captures what you believe God is saying through this passage. This disciplines your thinking and sharpens your message.

4. Compare translations. Reading the same passage in three translations will often unlock meaning that a single version obscures. Scripture Studio's 13-translation reader makes this effortless.

strong>5. Read it again tomorrow. Return to the passage the next day before you've written anything. You'll see something you missed. This second reading is often where sermons are born.

Understanding the Psalms: A Pastoral Guide

How to read, teach, and apply the Psalms in contemporary ministry settings with clarity and depth.

March 28, 2026 · 7 min read
▼

The Psalms are simultaneously the most personal and the most universal book in the Bible. They give language to every human emotion — grief, rage, worship, doubt, gratitude — and they show us that God is big enough to hold all of it.

Identify the Psalm type first. Is it a Psalm of lament? Praise? Wisdom? Ascent? Knowing the genre shapes how you read and teach it. Lament psalms, for example, always move toward trust even when they begin in despair.

Read them aloud. The Psalms were written to be sung. Reading them aloud — even in private — recovers their rhythm and emotional weight in a way silent reading cannot.

Let them teach your congregation to pray. One of the most powerful things you can do is assign a Psalm a week to your congregation and let them pray through it. Over time, you'll notice the language of the Psalms appearing in their own prayers.

Use Scripture Studio's Reading Plans to walk through the Psalms systematically. The 30-day Psalms & Proverbs plan is a powerful foundation for any congregation's prayer life.

New: Side-by-Side Translation Comparison

Compare any two Bible translations in the Commentaries view for richer exegetical insight during study.

March 18, 2026 · 3 min read
▼

We just shipped one of the most-requested features since launch: side-by-side Bible translation comparison inside the Commentaries page.

How it works: Open Commentaries, load any passage using the passage selector, and you'll see two translation dropdowns — Version A and Version B — above two scrollable panels. Each panel loads the live Bible text for that translation. Both fetch simultaneously so you're never waiting.

Why it matters: Translation comparison is one of the most powerful tools in exegesis, and it's been historically buried behind expensive software or clunky tab-switching. Now it's right next to the commentary, so you can read what Bishop Clark, Matthew Henry, or Jamieson says about a passage while simultaneously seeing how different translators rendered it.

All 13 translations are available in both panels. The feature works with any passage from Genesis 1 through Revelation 22.

Reading Plans and Congregational Growth

How structured reading plans build spiritual discipline and create shared language in your congregation.

March 10, 2026 · 6 min read
▼

One of the quietest but most powerful things a pastor can do for their congregation is give them a shared reading plan. When your people are all in the same passage, something remarkable happens to the culture of your church.

Shared language emerges. When your congregation is all reading the same passage, the conversations that happen in the lobby, in small groups, and on social media begin to use the same vocabulary. The Word becomes communal.

Accountability becomes natural. You don't have to engineer accountability when people are all on the same journey. The question "where are you in the plan?" becomes a natural part of church life.

Your preaching is amplified. When your congregation has been in a passage all week before you preach it Sunday, they arrive prepared. They've wrestled with the text. Your sermon answers questions they've already been asking.

Scripture Studio includes six structured reading plans. Start one personally, then recommend it to your congregation. The results tend to compound.
